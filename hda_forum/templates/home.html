NBN Network technology

  • Fibre to the premises (FTTP)
  • Fibre to the building/basement (FTTB)
  • Fibre to the cabinate (FTTC)
  • Fibre to the node (FTTN)
  • Hybrid fibre coaxial (HFC)
  • Fixed wireless technology

Fibre to the premises

The fibre architecture provides connectivity from each location via a Drop Fibre to a Network Access Point (NAP), through concentration points within Fibre Distribution Hubs (FDH) in Fibre Distribution Areas (FDA) and Fibre Serving Areas (FSA). Each Fibre Access Node (FAN) is connected to Transit Fibre to provide backhaul to the Point of Interconnection (POI) with the Retail Service Providers (RSP) in the nearest practical location The Aggregation Node will be co-located with the most appropriate FAN to allow the provision of competitive backhaul for the Retail and Wholesale Service Providers

In a FTTP implementation fiber runs all the way from the central office to each home and is clearly the end objective. FTTP architectures are future-safe, enabling operators to deliver unlimited bandwidth: 100 Mbps per household today, with technology evolutions set to increase this in the future. Their main drawback, however, is that (nationwide) FTTP deployments require a lot of time and money.

Fibre to the basement (FTTB)

Fibre optic cables connected from POI to Node which will be placed basement of the building, from there existing telstra copper cables are used to connect individual homes. FTTB is one step behind the FTTP footprint.

Fibre to the Cabinate (FTTC)

Fibre cables connected from POI to cabinate, which will be located very near to the premises maximum of 150 meters distances to the premises. which will enable high speed bandwidth. existing telstra copper cables will be used effectively to connect Cabinate to premises.

Fibre to the Node (FTTN)

Fibre cables connected from POI to Node, which will be located some where near to the premises maximum of 600 to 800 meters distances from the premises. Tie cables (C & X pair) will be used to connect from node to pillar (CCU), and existing telstra copper cables used to connect pillar to CTB (Connection Terminal Block) device.

Hybrid fibre Coaxial (HFC)

History of HFC

In the 1950s the first networks started deployment in the US. These networks were designed to receive a TV signal in a central antenna and distribute the television signal to local community. As the size of these networks grew, so did the technical challenges to deliver quality television. The solution was to use fibre as a means of pushing a high quality television signal much closer to the home. This became know as Hybrid Fibre Coax

HFC Roll Out in Australia

The early 1990s with the advent of deregulation, saw the entry of Optus into the Australian market. At the time Optus was part owned by an America company, CableVision. As part of its entry into the market Optus decided to build a HFC network. From this network they would offer both PayTV and Voice services Telstra in response to this entry then built its own HFC network, with a roll out schedule that largely followed that of Optus

how does it work?

The coax cable carries its own spectrum and the spectrum is then divided up into channels. Upstream Channels are separate to Downstream Channels. Some channels carry PayTV, other channels carry internet traffic High Speed Broadband is achieved by bonding multiple channels together

Fixed wireless

nbn Fixed Wireless is a new broadband technology that offers a much faster and more reliable internet connection that what is currently available in most regional areas. A small antenna installed on your premises picks up a signal from a wireless tower and allows you access to the internet. With speeds up to 25Mbps, the service is much faster than standard ADSL internet services available in metro areas.
